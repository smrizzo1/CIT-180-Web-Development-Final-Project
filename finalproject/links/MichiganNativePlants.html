Adding native plants and shrubs to the home garden enhances beauty while attracting local pollinators and wildlife

Enthusiasm has grown recently over utilizing native plants and shrubs in home gardening. If selected and placed properly, native plants generally suffer from less insect damage and disease than non-native, or "ornamental" plants, are minimally invasive, and "create the best environments for local pollinators and wildlife."

Because they are adapted to the local environment, native plants also tend to need less supplemental nutrition, water, and maintenance than ornamental plants, making them perfect for the "lazy" gardener. Although often not considered as pretty as the more exotic imports, a look at the beauties below may change your mind!11

Here are several popular native plants and shrubs for Michigan gardens - hover or tap to learn more:

Wild Blue Flag (Iris versicolor 1)
Sun: Full/Part
Moisture: Average/Moist
Height: 2-3 ft
Provides Food/Nectar
Downward-curving, violet-blue flowers with yellow-based sepals on a sturdy stalk. Sword-like leaves. Prefers wetlands. “Flag,” from the middle English “flagge,” means “rush” or “reed.”2 photo by: Bennie Bengton

Butterfly Weed (Asclepias tuberosa 1)
Sun: Full
Moisture: Dry/Average
Height: 1-3 ft
Larval Host for Butterflies, Provides Food/Nectar
Clusters of bright-orange flowers attracts bees and butterflies in abundance. Root used by Native Americans to treat pulmonary ailments.3 photo by: Alan Cressler

Wild Columbine(Aquilegia canadensis 1)
Sun: Full/Part/Shade
Moisture: Average
Height: 1-2 ft
Larval Host for Butterflies, Provides Food/Nectar
Showy, red and yellow flowers with spreading sepals and numerous yellow stamens. The narrow, tube-like flowers produce nectar especially favored by hummingbirds.4 photo by: Julie Makin

Wild Lupine (Lupinus perennis 1)
Sun: Full/Part
Moisture: Dry
Height: 1-2 ft
Larval Host for Butterflies, Provides Food/Nectar
Purple-blue clusters of flowers on elongated 1-2 ft stems. Once thought to “wolf” (or deplete) minerals from soil, it is now known to enhance soil fertility by fixing nitrogen from the atmosphere to the soil.6 photo by: W.D and Dolphia Bransford

Cardinal Flower (Lobelia cardinalis 1)
Sun: Full/Part
Moisture: Average/Moist
Height: 2-3 ft
Provides Food/Nectar
Bright red, spiky flowers with two upper petals and three lower petals spreading from a basal tube. Erect stems lined with lance-shaped leaves. Attracts hummingbirds. Named for the red robes worn by Roman Catholic cardinals.5 photo by: Norman G. Flaigg

Wild Bergamot (Monarda fistulosa 1)
Sun: Full
Moisture: Dry/Average
Height: 2-4 ft
Provides Food/Nectar
Also known as Beebalm, clusters of lavender flowers resemble ragged pompoms. Open branched stems with aromatic, minty-smelling leaves used to make tea. The oil was used for respiratory ailments.7 photo by: Mary Ann Pickens

Serviceberry (Amelanchier arboria 1)
Sun: Full/Part
Moisture: Dry/Average
Height: 12-25 ft
Larval Host for Butterflies, Provides Food/Nectar
White flowers that appear before the leaves. Produces edible, reddish-purple berries. Also called Shadbush since the flowers bloom around the time shad swim upriver to spawn.8 photo by: Thomas L. Muller

Winterberry (Prunus virginiana 1)
Sun: Full/Part
Moisture: Average/Moist
Height: 6-12 ft
Provides Provides Food/Nectar
Also known as Michigan Holly. Inconspicuous flowers produce bright red berries that remain through the winter, attracting birds. 9 photo by: George H. Bruso

Choke Cherry (Prunus virginiana 1)
Sun: Full/Part
Moisture: Average/Moist
Height: 10-30 ft
Larval Host for Butterflies, Provides Provides Food/Nectar
White flowers in dense clusters produce dark purple berries. Though “puckery” when immature, the berries are often made into jelly when mature.10 photo by: Sally and Andy Wasowski
